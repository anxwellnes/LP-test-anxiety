Productive worry style
Your worry mostly works for you
Your results suggest your worry style is primarily productive: it tends to lead to action, stay reasonably calibrated, and resolve when the concern is addressed. You worry in a way that serves you rather than trapping you.
Keep this in mind
Productive worry can become unproductive under high stress, sleep deprivation or when concerns are genuinely unresolvable. Noticing when the worry stops producing action and starts cycling is the key signal to change approach.

The key distinction
The question to ask each time you notice yourself worrying is: is there a concrete action I can take based on this concern right now? If yes, take it. If no, the thinking is not serving you in this moment.
The most effective technique
Scheduled worry time, containing worry to a specific 15-20 minute window, helps distinguish productive concerns that generate plans from circular ones that merely generate more worry.

Why unproductive worry persists
Unproductive worry is maintained partly by positive beliefs about worry: the idea that worrying more means you are taking things seriously, that worrying is preparation, that if you stop worrying something bad will happen. These beliefs are worth examining directly.
The cost at this level
Predominantly unproductive worry consumes cognitive resource without producing the preparation it seems to promise. It impairs decision-making by adding emotional weight to every option, disrupts sleep and reduces present-moment quality of life.
What changes this
CBT directly targets the beliefs about worry and the behaviours that maintain unproductive thinking. The scheduled worry technique, the usefulness question and cognitive defusion are the core tools.
