How avoidance works against you

Every avoided situation teaches the nervous system that the situation was genuinely dangerous, which increases the anxiety about it next time. Avoidance provides short-term relief at the cost of long-term maintenance and expansion of the anxiety.

Where to start

The most effective starting point is identifying the least costly avoidance to reduce, the situation that would be slightly uncomfortable to re-engage with but not overwhelming, and doing that first. Each successful re-engagement provides evidence against the anxiety predictions.

The compounding problem

At this level, avoidance is compounding: each avoided situation makes the next approach more difficult, the range of comfortable activity narrows year by year, and the anxiety becomes increasingly resistant to natural resolution because it is never tested against reality.

Exposure is the specific intervention

Exposure therapy, systematically and gradually re-engaging with avoided situations starting from the least threatening, is the most evidence-based intervention for avoidance-maintained anxiety. It is the only approach that directly reverses the maintaining mechanism.

Working with a therapist

Significant avoidance at this level is most effectively addressed with the support of a therapist who can help structure the exposure hierarchy and provide guidance through the temporarily increased anxiety that exposure work involves.
