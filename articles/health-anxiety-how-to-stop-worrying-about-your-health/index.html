Health Anxiety: How to Stop Worrying About Your Health

📖 10 min read 🧠 MyAnxietyTest

Health anxiety is the persistent and distressing preoccupation with having or developing a serious illness, in the absence of medical evidence to support the fear. It used to be called hypochondria, a term that has largely been abandoned because of its dismissive connotations.

Health anxiety is not about attention-seeking or weakness. It is a specific and well-understood pattern in which normal body sensations are interpreted as evidence of serious illness, and in which behaviours that feel protective, checking, seeking reassurance, avoiding health information or obsessively researching symptoms, actually maintain and intensify the fear.

This guide explains what health anxiety is, why it persists and what actually works for reducing it.

What health anxiety actually involves

Health anxiety sits on a spectrum. At the mild end, it involves occasional worry about health that is disproportionate to the actual risk. At the more severe end, it involves persistent, near-daily preoccupation with serious illness that significantly affects daily functioning, relationships and quality of life.

The core feature is not the presence of physical symptoms but the interpretation of those symptoms. Most people with health anxiety are not imagining their symptoms. They are experiencing real physical sensations, the racing heart, the headache, the stomach pain, the fatigue, but interpreting them through a lens that makes them evidence of serious illness rather than normal variations in bodily experience.

The body produces an enormous range of sensations that most people notice briefly and move past. For someone with health anxiety, these sensations become focal points that demand explanation and generate cycles of concern that are difficult to interrupt.

The maintaining cycle: why health anxiety does not resolve with reassurance

Health anxiety is maintained by a set of responses that feel logical but that prevent the anxiety from ever resolving.

Checking behaviours: examining the body for lumps, measuring pulse, inspecting moles, monitoring breathing. Each check is an implicit test of the feared hypothesis, and the temporary relief it provides is quickly replaced by doubt and the need to check again.

Reassurance-seeking: visiting doctors repeatedly, searching symptoms online, asking family members whether they think you seem unwell. Reassurance provides relief for approximately 10 to 20 minutes before the doubt returns, often more intensely.

Avoidance: avoiding health information, medical check-ups, conversations about illness, or activities that produce bodily sensations associated with the feared illness. Avoidance maintains the sense of threat and prevents the anxiety from updating.

The pattern is self-reinforcing. The behaviours that provide short-term relief maintain the long-term anxiety, which drives more of the behaviours.

Body scanning and symptom monitoring

One of the most important maintaining mechanisms in health anxiety is heightened interoceptive attention, or body scanning. This is the practice of monitoring the body for sensations that might indicate illness.

The problem is that the more closely you monitor your body, the more sensations you find. The body is always producing a huge range of normal sensations that most people never notice because they are not attending to them. Selective attention toward these sensations amplifies them, makes them more prominent and more interpretable as evidence of illness.

Health anxiety often involves a feedback loop: the anxiety produces physical symptoms through the stress response, including muscle tension, palpitations, digestive disturbance and fatigue, and these stress-induced symptoms are then interpreted as evidence of the illness the person is anxious about. The anxiety is literally producing the symptoms it fears.

What works: evidence-based approaches

CBT has the strongest evidence base for health anxiety. The most effective components are:

Reducing checking and reassurance-seeking. This is the most direct intervention. Every check feeds the cycle. Reducing checking systematically, starting with the most frequent and least distressing, directly interrupts the maintaining mechanism. This is uncomfortable initially because the anxiety spikes without the usual relief, but it extinguishes quickly.

Attention refocusing. Deliberately practising external focus rather than body monitoring. This does not mean ignoring the body but choosing where to direct attention. Engaging in absorbing activities, particularly physical or social ones, reduces the amount of cognitive resource available for body scanning.

Cognitive restructuring. Examining the evidence for the catastrophic interpretation versus more benign explanations of the sensations. Developing a more accurate understanding of how common normal bodily sensations are and how rarely they indicate serious illness.

Exposure to health-related information and situations. For people who have been avoiding medical information, health conversations or physical activities associated with their feared illness, gradual exposure reduces the avoidance-maintained threat.

When to see a doctor and when not to

A reasonable approach for people with health anxiety is to have a clear agreement with a doctor about the frequency and criteria for seeking medical reassurance. This might mean one assessment of a given symptom, with the agreement that if the assessment is clear, no further testing will be sought for that symptom for a defined period.

Seeking repeated medical reassurance beyond this agreement feeds the cycle rather than resolving it. Doctors can and should assess physical symptoms appropriately, but repeated reassurance-seeking beyond what the clinical picture warrants maintains rather than resolves health anxiety.

This is not about dismissing genuine health concerns. It is about recognising when the pattern of seeking reassurance has become the maintaining mechanism rather than the solution.

Frequently asked questions
Is health anxiety a mental illness?+

Health anxiety disorder is a recognised clinical condition in the DSM-5, classified as somatic symptom disorder or illness anxiety disorder depending on the specific presentation. This does not mean it is permanent or untreatable. CBT has strong evidence for producing significant and lasting improvement.

Can health anxiety cause real physical symptoms?+

Yes. Anxiety activates the sympathetic nervous system, which produces real physical symptoms including palpitations, chest tightness, shortness of breath, dizziness, nausea, muscle tension and fatigue. These symptoms are genuinely physical, not imagined, but they are produced by the anxiety response rather than by the illness being feared.

Does googling symptoms make health anxiety worse?+

Consistently, yes. Symptom searching almost always returns serious or rare conditions as possible explanations, because medical information is written to cover all possibilities. For someone with health anxiety, this provides a continuous supply of threatening possibilities that fuel the anxiety cycle. Reducing symptom searching is one of the most direct and effective interventions.

How is health anxiety different from hypochondria?+

Hypochondria was the older term for what is now called health anxiety disorder or illness anxiety disorder. The older term has been largely abandoned because of its dismissive and stigmatising connotations. The clinical pattern being described is the same.
