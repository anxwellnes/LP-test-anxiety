How to Find a Therapist for Anxiety: A Practical Guide

📖 8 min read 🧠 MyAnxietyTest

Deciding to find a therapist is one of the most useful things you can do for anxiety. The process of actually finding one can feel daunting, particularly when anxiety itself makes uncertainty and new situations harder to navigate. This guide makes it as straightforward as possible.

Know what you are looking for before you start

The most important filter when looking for a therapist for anxiety is their approach. Not all therapists use the same methods, and the evidence is clear that some approaches work significantly better for anxiety than others.

Cognitive Behavioural Therapy, CBT, has the strongest evidence base for anxiety and is the approach most widely recommended by clinical guidelines. Exposure-based therapy, which is a component of CBT, is particularly effective when avoidance has become significant. Acceptance and Commitment Therapy, ACT, is also widely supported and works particularly well for people who have found that trying to control anxious thoughts makes things worse.

When searching, look for therapists who list CBT, ACT or exposure therapy as their primary approaches. General counselling and psychodynamic approaches can be helpful but have less specific evidence for anxiety.

Where to search

  • Online therapy platforms offer the most accessible route to a qualified therapist. They allow you to browse therapist profiles, read about their approach and specialisations, and start without having to travel or navigate a new environment
  • Your GP or primary care physician can provide a referral and may have access to therapy services through the healthcare system
  • Psychology Today's therapist finder is a widely used directory that allows you to filter by location, approach, specialisation and whether they offer online sessions
  • University training clinics often offer reduced-cost therapy with supervised trainee therapists whose work is of good quality
  • Your workplace may offer an Employee Assistance Programme that includes a number of free therapy sessions

What to look for in a therapist profile

  • Specific mention of anxiety as a specialisation, not just a general list of everything they work with
  • Named therapeutic approaches that have evidence for anxiety, particularly CBT, ACT or exposure-based work
  • Relevant qualifications and registration with a professional body
  • Whether they offer online sessions if that is important to you
  • A profile that gives you a sense of how they work and communicate, not just a list of credentials

The first session is not a commitment

One of the most common barriers to finding a therapist is the feeling that choosing one is a big, binding decision. It is not. The first session is an assessment, not a contract. Its purpose is for both you and the therapist to understand what is going on and whether working together feels like a good fit.

It is entirely appropriate to see two or three therapists before deciding who to work with. The therapeutic relationship is one of the strongest predictors of outcomes in therapy, and finding someone you feel comfortable with is worth the effort of an initial search. Most people find the first session significantly less daunting than they anticipated.

Online therapy is a legitimate and accessible option

For many people with anxiety, the practical and psychological barriers to starting in-person therapy are significant. Online therapy removes most of them. You do not need to travel to an unfamiliar location, sit in a waiting room or navigate a new social environment. You can access support from wherever you feel most comfortable.

Research consistently shows that online therapy is as effective as in-person therapy for anxiety. The therapeutic relationship, which is the most important factor in outcomes, develops just as effectively in online sessions. If the lower barrier to starting means you actually start, that is a significant practical advantage.
