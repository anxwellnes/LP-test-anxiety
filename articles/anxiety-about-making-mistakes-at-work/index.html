Anxiety About Making Mistakes at Work: Why It Happens and What Helps

Updated March 2026 · 10 min read

Almost everyone feels some concern about making mistakes at work. That is normal and probably useful. The version that becomes a problem is different in character: it is disproportionate to actual risk, it persists long after a mistake has been corrected, it causes you to over-check your work for hours, and it sometimes stops you from attempting things at all because the possibility of getting it wrong feels intolerable.

If that description resonates, you are dealing with anxiety about mistakes rather than healthy professional concern. The distinction matters because they respond to different things.

Why some people's brains treat mistakes as catastrophic

The anxiety response exists to protect us from threats. For much of human history, serious mistakes had serious consequences: misjudge a situation and you might be injured, rejected from the group, or lose access to resources that mattered. The brain evolved to take mistakes very seriously.

For most modern workplace mistakes, the actual consequences are much smaller than the threat response. A wrong figure in a report, an email sent to the wrong person, a task delivered late: these are correctable errors with manageable consequences. But the anxious brain does not automatically scale its response to the actual level of risk. It scales it to the perceived threat level, which is driven by thoughts rather than facts.

The cognitive distortions that drive mistake anxiety at work

The specific thought patterns that maintain this type of anxiety are well-documented in CBT literature. Recognising which ones are active in your own thinking is the first step toward changing the response.

Catastrophising

"If I get this wrong, I could lose my job, and then I wouldn't be able to pay my rent, and then..." The mind jumps to the worst plausible outcome and treats it as the likely one. The chain of consequences extends far beyond what the situation actually supports.

All-or-nothing thinking

Mistakes feel like total failure rather than partial errors. "I made a mistake in this presentation" becomes "I am bad at my job." There is no middle ground between perfect and worthless.

Mind reading

"My manager thinks I'm incompetent." "My colleagues have noticed and are judging me." These are guesses presented to yourself as facts. The anxiety then responds to the guess as if it were confirmed.

Overgeneralisation

One mistake becomes evidence of a pattern: "I always do this," "I'm never going to get better at this." Single events get treated as proof of a permanent trait.

The avoidance trap

Mistake anxiety often creates avoidance patterns that feel protective but actually maintain the anxiety. Common ones include: spending significantly more time checking work than the task warrants, seeking repeated reassurance from colleagues or managers, avoiding tasks with higher stakes even when you are qualified to do them, and procrastinating on tasks where the possibility of being wrong feels threatening.

These behaviours reduce anxiety in the short term. They also prevent you from accumulating evidence that mistakes are manageable, which keeps the fear intact. The more you over-check, the more you communicate to your brain that the situation warranted the extra checking. The anxiety is reinforced rather than reduced.

Strategies that actually help

1

Separate the error from the interpretation

When a mistake happens, try to distinguish between what actually occurred and what you are telling yourself about it. "I submitted the wrong file" is a fact. "I am incompetent and my manager thinks I'm useless" is an interpretation. Work with the fact. Challenge the interpretation.

2

Do a realistic consequences audit

Ask yourself: what is the actual, most likely outcome of this mistake? Not the worst plausible outcome, but the realistic one. Most workplace errors are correctable. Most managers are more interested in how you handle a mistake than in the mistake itself. Running the realistic numbers counteracts the catastrophising.

3

Set a checking limit and stick to it

If you over-check your work, decide in advance how many times you will review something for a given type of task. One review for a routine email. Two reviews for a complex report. Stopping at the agreed limit, even when anxiety pushes you to check again, gradually teaches your brain that the unchecked version was fine.

4

Notice when you are seeking reassurance compulsively

Asking for feedback is healthy. Asking multiple people whether a piece of work is acceptable because you cannot tolerate the uncertainty of not knowing is reassurance-seeking. The latter maintains anxiety. Try to notice the difference and gradually reduce the reassurance-seeking pattern.

5

Process mistakes deliberately rather than ruminating

Give yourself a defined window, say 20 minutes, to think about what went wrong and what you would do differently. Then close that window. Rumination loops that run for hours do not produce better learning than deliberate, time-limited reflection. They produce more anxiety.

When self-help is not enough

If anxiety about mistakes at work is significantly affecting your performance, causing you to avoid responsibilities, or producing physical symptoms like sleep disruption, chest tightness, or persistent dread, self-help strategies are a useful starting point but not a sufficient endpoint. The thought patterns behind this kind of anxiety are well-treated by CBT, and working through them with a therapist who can personalise the approach to your specific patterns tends to produce faster and more durable change than working through them alone.

The role anxiety plays in high-performing people

It is worth noting that anxiety about mistakes is particularly common in people who are actually competent and conscientious. The people least concerned about making mistakes are often those with the least self-awareness about their performance. Mistake anxiety tends to be highest in people who care most about doing their work well.

That does not make it easier to live with. But it does mean the target of treatment is not making you care less about your work. It is recalibrating how your nervous system responds to the normal uncertainty that comes with doing work that matters to you.

That recalibration is exactly what CBT is designed for, and it is something most people with this pattern can make real progress on with the right support.
