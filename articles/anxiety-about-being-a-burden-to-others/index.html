Anxiety About Being a Burden to Others: Why It Happens and What Helps

Updated March 2026 · 10 min read

The feeling of being a burden is one of the more quietly painful aspects of living with anxiety. It tends to arrive when you most need support: when you are struggling, when you want to ask for help, when you need to cancel plans because you are not well enough to keep them. And it tells you that reaching out will cost the people you care about more than it is worth.

This is not an obscure or unusual experience. It is extremely common in people with anxiety, and it has specific psychological roots that are worth understanding, because the belief that you are a burden is almost never as accurate as it feels.

"The feeling of being a burden is often loudest precisely when you most need support. That is not a coincidence."

Where the belief comes from

The belief that you are a burden tends to have roots in early experiences of emotional caregiving, criticism for having needs, or environments where expressing difficulty was met with irritation, dismissal, or withdrawal. When asking for help or showing vulnerability consistently resulted in a negative response, the nervous system learned to associate need with threat. The belief that you are a burden is the cognitive version of that learned pattern.

It is also common in people who grew up in households where being useful or capable was closely tied to being valued. If your worth was conditional on not being difficult, not needing too much, or managing without help, then needing support as an adult gets entangled with deep fears about being less valuable or less loveable.

How anxiety amplifies it

Anxiety has a well-documented tendency to amplify negative predictions and discount contradicting evidence. The belief that you are a burden gets treated by the anxious mind as established fact rather than as a hypothesis worth examining. Situations that could be read as evidence of someone caring about you get filtered out. Anything that could be read as confirming the burden belief gets amplified and retained.

This selective attention maintains the belief even in relationships where the actual evidence does not support it. The people who care about you, and who would genuinely want to know when you are struggling, are invisible through the lens of the burden belief.

The ways it shows up as behaviour

Not asking for help when you need it

The most direct expression of the burden belief. You manage alone, even when managing alone is causing significant distress, because asking feels like imposing. The result is that the people close to you do not know you are struggling, which in turn can produce real distance that feels like confirmation of the original belief.

Apologising excessively for your needs

When you do ask for something, it comes wrapped in so many apologies and minimisations that the other person is left uncertain about what you actually need. The hedging reduces the chance of the interaction feeling positive, which again can seem to confirm that asking was a mistake.

Withdrawing when you are struggling

Rather than risk being a burden, you disappear. You cancel plans, go quiet on messages, become less present. From the other person's perspective, this can look like disinterest or withdrawal, producing exactly the relational distance the burden belief was trying to prevent.

Being excessively helpful to justify your presence

Some people respond to the burden belief by trying to be maximally useful, giving a great deal so that they feel they have earned the right to need anything at all. This over-giving is exhausting, creates an unbalanced dynamic, and does not actually address the underlying belief.

What makes this hard to challenge alone

The challenge with the burden belief is that the behaviours it produces, withdrawing, not asking for help, over-giving, tend to create the relational outcomes that confirm it. If you withdraw when struggling, you do not get the experience of someone showing up for you that would challenge the belief. If you never ask for help, you never find out that asking was fine.

This is why talking yourself out of the belief is less effective than gradually changing the behaviour and accumulating different experiences. That is a process that tends to work better with professional support, because a therapist can help you understand the roots of the belief, identify the behavioural patterns maintaining it, and design small, carefully paced experiments in asking for help and staying present that build new evidence over time.

A reframe worth sitting with

The people who love you and who you worry about burdening, how do you feel when they come to you with something difficult? Do you feel burdened? Or do you feel trusted, useful, close to them in a way that matters?

Most people, when they think about it honestly, find that the experience of someone they care about sharing their struggles feels like intimacy, not imposition. The belief that you are uniquely burdensome to others often does not survive the direct examination of how you yourself experience being needed by people you care about.

That does not make the feeling disappear. But it is a starting point for holding the belief a little more loosely.

When to seek support

If the belief that you are a burden is significantly affecting your relationships, causing you to suffer in silence more often than not, or making it very hard to seek help even when you recognise that you need it, working through it with a therapist is likely to produce more change than working through it alone. This is exactly the kind of pattern that CBT and relational therapeutic approaches address directly.
