Anxiety or Intuition: How to Tell the Difference

📖 11 min read🧠 MyAnxietyTest📅 May 2026

Something feels wrong, but you cannot say what. Is it your gut telling you something real, or is it anxiety disguising itself as wisdom? This question matters more than it might seem. If you mistake anxiety for intuition, you make decisions based on fear rather than information. If you dismiss genuine intuition as anxiety, you override something useful. Both are costly. Here is how to tell them apart with enough reliability to trust your own signals again.

The five questions
Apply these to your current feeling to tell which is more likely operating
1. When you sit quietly with the feeling, does it stay calm or become more urgent?
Points to intuition
The feeling remains present and calm in quiet. It does not escalate. It simply waits, clear and stable, without demanding immediate action.
Points to anxiety
The feeling escalates when given attention. Quiet amplifies rather than clarifies it. The urgency increases the longer you sit with it without acting.
2. Is the feeling specific to this situation or person, or is it your general state right now?
Points to intuition
The feeling is clearly about this specific situation, decision or person. It is not present in other areas of your life right now.
Points to anxiety
You are also anxious about several other things right now. This feeling is not distinct from your general current state. Anxiety has attached to this particular object.
3. If the concern this feeling points to resolved tomorrow, what would happen to the feeling?
Points to intuition
The feeling would resolve proportionately. If the situation it is pointing to is addressed, the feeling should no longer need to be present.
Points to anxiety
Another concern would likely arise to take its place. Anxiety shifts targets. The feeling attaches to whatever is available, not to the source of genuine information.
4. How is the feeling when you are well-rested and low-stress compared to when you are tired and overwhelmed?
Points to intuition
Genuine intuition is more accessible when calm and rested. It does not dramatically change in intensity based on your current stress level.
Points to anxiety
The feeling is significantly more intense when tired, stressed or low. If it is much worse in those states, it is reflecting your current anxiety level, not objective information.
5. Does the feeling come with physical urgency and alarm, or with a quiet knowing?
Points to intuition
A quiet sense of knowing, often described as calm certainty. Present without demanding immediate action. Not accompanied by racing heart or panic.
Points to anxiety
Physical urgency: racing heart, tightness, a sense that something bad is imminent. Anxiety activates the full stress response. Genuine intuition typically does not.
Why anxiety is so convincing as intuition
Anxiety uses the same physiological channels as genuine intuition. Both produce bodily sensations, both arrive below conscious reasoning, both feel like meaningful information rather than random noise. The difference is that genuine intuition is pattern recognition based on real information, while anxiety is a threat-detection system generating signals without requiring proportionate evidence. When you have had anxiety for a long time, you have had a very loud signal in those channels for a very long time. Learning to tell the difference takes practice, and a therapist is the most effective guide for that practice.
What it costs
The specific ways mistaking anxiety for intuition affects your decisions and your life

When anxiety is consistently mistaken for intuition, decisions get made based on fear signals rather than genuine information. Relationships are ended because the anxiety felt like a warning. Opportunities are declined because the anxiety felt like a signal to stop. Situations are avoided because the discomfort of anxiety was interpreted as the body saying "danger" when it was actually saying "threat-detection system currently overactive."

Over time, this pattern can produce a life shaped significantly by anxiety's preferences rather than your own. The anxiety has been consulted as an oracle. It is not an oracle. It is a sensitised alarm system that sounds for reasons unrelated to the actual level of danger in your current situation.

This is particularly costly in relationships. Anxiety in relationships frequently manifests as gut feelings about a partner's feelings, intentions or faithfulness that are generated by the anxiety system rather than by observable reality. Acting on these anxiety-signals as though they were reliable intuition causes the relational damage the anxiety was predicting, through the anxiety-driven behaviour, rather than through any actual problem in the relationship.

Conversely, when genuine intuition is dismissed as anxiety, you override something worth attending to. The goal is not to stop trusting your gut. It is to develop the ability to tell which signal you are receiving, so that your gut can be trusted again with confidence.
