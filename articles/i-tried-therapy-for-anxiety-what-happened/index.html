I Tried Therapy for My Anxiety: Here Is What Actually Happened

Updated March 2026 · 11 min read · Personal account

For about six years, I managed my anxiety the way most people do: carefully, quietly, and alone. I avoided situations that triggered it, built routines around minimising it, and told myself it was just how I was wired. Anxious people existed everywhere. Most of them were fine. I was fine.

Except I was spending roughly two hours a day caught in loops of worst-case thinking, cancelling social plans once a fortnight, and waking up at 4am with a chest that felt like something heavy was sitting on it. That is not fine. It is managed. There is a difference.

I started therapy at 31, a full decade after I first noticed my anxiety was affecting my life. This is the story of what happened, what surprised me, and what I would tell my younger self now.

"I thought therapy was for people whose anxiety was bad enough to count. Mine was bad enough. I had just gotten very good at not counting it."

The barrier that kept me from starting

The thing I kept telling myself was that my anxiety was not bad enough. I was functional. I held a job. I had relationships. There was always someone worse off, and I had internalised this idea that therapy was a resource you saved until you genuinely could not cope.

That belief is wrong, and it is also extremely common. Anxiety does not need to be disabling to merit treatment. It needs to be affecting your life in ways that you would prefer it did not. That was true for me for a long time before I did anything about it.

The second barrier was less philosophical and more practical. I did not know how to find a therapist, what to say in a first session, or what I was actually hoping for. The whole thing felt opaque and effortful in a way that felt particularly unfair when anxiety was the problem I was trying to solve. Being anxious about starting anxiety therapy is its own specific comedy.

How I finally started

I started through an online platform rather than in-person therapy. The lower friction was the deciding factor. No commute, no waiting room, no phone call to book an appointment. I signed up on a Sunday evening when I was already on my laptop, which meant the window where I might have talked myself out of it stayed closed.

My first session was a video call. My therapist asked me what brought me there. I had expected a formal intake questionnaire but it was more like a conversation. She asked good questions. I talked more than I expected to.

What the first month looked like

1

Week 1: Being asked to describe my anxiety in detail

I had never really articulated what my anxiety felt like, just lived it. Being asked to describe the physical sensations, the thought patterns, and the situations that triggered it was oddly clarifying. I had never mapped it before.

2

Week 2: Starting thought records

My therapist introduced thought records. You write down the situation, your automatic thought, the emotion and its intensity, then challenge the thought with evidence. It felt mechanical at first. I kept doing it anyway.

3

Week 3: The first moment something clicked

I wrote down a thought during an anxiety spike and looked at the evidence column. The evidence against the catastrophic thought was overwhelming and I had never once in six years thought to look for it. That was genuinely strange to sit with.

4

Week 4: Noticing the patterns

After a month of thought records, I could see that 80 percent of my anxiety clustered around two specific themes. My therapist pointed this out before I did. Having a pattern to work on, rather than a diffuse general feeling, made the problem feel smaller and more tractable.

What actually changed

The changes were not dramatic. There was no single session that transformed everything. What happened was more like a gradual recalibration. The 4am chest-heaviness became less frequent, then rare. The thought loops shortened. I started catching myself mid-spiral and doing something different instead of riding it out.

The most significant change was harder to name. I stopped relating to my anxiety as something happening to me and started relating to it as something I had some influence over. That shift in relationship to the anxiety, not its disappearance, was the actual outcome of therapy. The anxiety still shows up. I am just considerably better at being with it.

"The anxiety still shows up. I am just considerably better at being with it."

What I wish I had known before starting

The first session is mostly intake. Do not expect breakthroughs in the first hour. Go in willing to be asked a lot of questions and to talk more than feels comfortable.

Therapy works better when you do the homework. This is not a passive process where you show up and get fixed. The sessions are the map. The week between sessions is where you actually walk the territory.

You do not have to stay with the first therapist you are matched with. Fit matters. If something feels off after two or three sessions, say so or request a change. A mismatched therapist is not evidence that therapy does not work for you.

Progress is not linear. There will be sessions that feel like nothing happened, and weeks where your anxiety spikes in the middle of a structured program. This is normal and does not mean you are failing.

On waiting a decade

I am not here to tell you that I wasted ten years. The coping strategies I built were not worthless. Some of them I still use. But structured support with an actual framework, a therapist who understood the mechanics of anxiety, and a set of tools that addressed the patterns rather than just managing the symptoms: that was available to me a decade earlier than I accessed it. That is the part I find harder to sit with.

If you are in a similar position, sitting with anxiety that is managed rather than actually addressed, the gap between those two things is where therapy lives.
