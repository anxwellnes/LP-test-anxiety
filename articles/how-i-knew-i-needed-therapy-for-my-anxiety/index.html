How I Knew I Needed Therapy for My Anxiety: The Moments That Made It Clear

Updated March 2026 · 10 min read · Personal account

I did not have a single dramatic breakdown that made the decision obvious. I think a lot of people expect that, and when it does not happen, they conclude that their situation does not meet the threshold. That their anxiety is something they should be able to handle with enough willpower, information, and the right breathing techniques.

The moments that made it clear for me were quieter than that. They accumulated. And looking back at them now, from the other side of having actually gone to therapy, I can see that each one of them was evidence I was not willing to look at directly at the time.

I am writing this because I recognise these moments in the accounts of a lot of people who are still in that place, managing anxiety that is more than they should have to manage alone, and not yet willing to call it enough.

The moments

Moment 1

I started calculating the anxiety cost of everything

Not consciously, at first. But I noticed that before agreeing to do almost anything, I would run a background calculation: how anxious will this make me, how much recovery time will I need, is this worth it? The calculation was automatic, and it was slowly narrowing my life. I was not choosing things I wanted. I was choosing things that my anxiety could afford.

Moment 2

My coping strategies were taking more time than the anxiety itself

I had built a fairly elaborate set of routines designed to keep anxiety manageable. Specific sleep times, specific morning rituals, specific rules about when I could check email and how I had to prepare for meetings. When I added up the time these strategies took, it was significant. My life had been organised around anxiety management to a degree I had not admitted to myself.

Moment 3

I realised I had stopped telling people when I was struggling

I could not remember the last time I had told a friend or partner I was having a bad anxiety week without immediately minimising it. I had become so skilled at appearing fine that even people close to me had no accurate picture of what I was carrying. That level of concealment takes energy. And I realised I was exhausted from it.

"I had become so skilled at appearing fine that even the people closest to me had no accurate picture of what I was carrying."

Moment 4

The self-help was no longer moving the needle

I had read the books. I had tried the apps. I understood CBT well enough to explain it to someone else. And my anxiety had not changed in a meaningful way in two years. There is a difference between understanding tools and having someone help you use them on the specific patterns in your specific life. I had reached the limit of what I could do with information alone.

Moment 5

I caught myself hoping something would cancel

Not every plan, but enough of them. I would agree to things and then quietly hope that something would come up to make them not happen. When that became a reliable pattern rather than an occasional one, I had to notice it. The avoidance had become default rather than exceptional.

Moment 6

My anxiety had started affecting other people

The person I was with at the time said something to me carefully, and kindly, about noticing how much I seemed to struggle with uncertainty in our shared plans. They were not criticising me. They were just observing something I had been hoping they had not noticed. Anxiety that affects only you is one thing. Anxiety that starts to shape the lives of people around you is something else.

What I told myself to stay where I was

I told myself I was managing. Which was true, in the narrowest sense. I told myself I was too busy, which was also technically true. I told myself that therapy was for people in crisis, which is not true but is a very common belief. I told myself that I already knew what CBT involved, so what would a therapist add.

Every one of these was a reasonable-sounding reason that was really a way of avoiding something I found difficult to face: that my anxiety had a hold on my life that was bigger than I wanted to admit, and that admitting it meant having to do something about it.

What changed after I started

It did not transform immediately. The first two sessions were mostly context-building. By the fourth session, I was doing thought records and noticing things about my own patterns that I had been too close to see. By three months in, the automatic anxiety-cost calculation before plans had noticeably reduced in frequency. By six months, my relationship with uncertainty was different in a way that was hard to describe to people who had not been through it.

Not fixed. Different. Which is a more honest description of what therapy produces.

The question I would ask yourself

Not whether your anxiety is bad enough. It probably is. The question is whether the version of your life you are living now, the one shaped around anxiety management, is the version you want to keep living. If the answer has any hesitation in it, that hesitation is worth paying attention to.

Therapy does not promise a life without anxiety. What it offers is a different relationship with the anxiety you have: one with more room in it for the things you actually want, and less of your energy going toward maintenance.

That is what I found on the other side. It took longer than I would have liked to walk toward it. I would start sooner if I were doing it again.
