The criteria
What the best online therapy for anxiety must actually have
🧠
CBT specifically, not just general therapy
Anxiety disorders respond to Cognitive Behavioural Therapy. The clinical evidence for CBT is stronger than for any other psychological approach for anxiety. A platform that offers general talk therapy or supportive counselling without a CBT structure is not delivering the most effective intervention for anxiety. The best platform for anxiety uses CBT as the foundation, not as an optional feature.
📋
A structured programme, not just open sessions
The most effective CBT for anxiety is delivered through a structured sequence: cognitive work first, then graduated exposure, with between-session exercises. Open-ended talk therapy sessions without structure may feel supportive but do not reliably produce the changes that structured CBT produces. The best platform provides worksheets, exercises and a programme that progresses between sessions.
🎓
Licensed therapists, not coaches or counsellors
Treating anxiety disorders requires a licensed clinician. Wellness coaches and counsellors without clinical licensure are not equipped to deliver CBT for clinical anxiety. The best platform employs therapists who are licensed to practise in your jurisdiction and have the training to deliver evidence-based anxiety treatment.
⚡
Fast access, not a 6-week waiting list
One of the main advantages of online therapy for anxiety is speed of access. In-person therapy typically has waiting times of 6 to 12 weeks. The best online therapy platform matches you with a therapist within 24 to 48 hours. This matters because anxiety that goes untreated for 6 more weeks while waiting continues to progress.

The evidence for online CBT for anxiety is now robust. Multiple meta-analyses have found that internet-delivered CBT produces outcomes equivalent to face-to-face CBT for generalised anxiety disorder, social anxiety, panic disorder and health anxiety. The therapeutic relationship, while different in format from in-person therapy, is sufficient to support the treatment process. The CBT techniques and the exposure work are as effective online as in person.

What the evidence does not support is general online counselling or coaching as equivalent to CBT for anxiety. The outcome equivalence applies specifically to CBT-based online therapy, not to general supportive online therapy. This is why the choice of platform matters: a platform that delivers structured CBT is delivering what the evidence supports, and a platform that delivers general support is not.
