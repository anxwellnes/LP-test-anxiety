CBT for Anxiety: What It Is and How It Actually Works

📖 8 min read 🧠 MyAnxietyTest

Cognitive Behavioural Therapy, CBT, is recommended in clinical guidelines around the world as the first-line psychological treatment for anxiety disorders. If you are considering therapy for anxiety, you will almost certainly encounter CBT. Here is what it actually is, how it works and what to expect from it.

What CBT is based on

CBT is based on the observation that anxiety is maintained by the interaction between thoughts, behaviours and physical sensations in a self-reinforcing cycle. The anxious thought, for example, something bad is going to happen, produces physical activation, which produces a behaviour such as avoidance or reassurance-seeking, which temporarily reduces the anxiety but reinforces the thought and makes the next episode more likely.

CBT works by identifying and changing the specific thoughts and behaviours that are maintaining the anxiety cycle, rather than simply managing the symptoms of anxiety as they arise.

The two main components

The cognitive component of CBT involves identifying the specific thoughts and beliefs that are driving the anxiety and examining them carefully. This does not mean telling yourself to think positively. It means developing the ability to notice anxious thoughts as thoughts rather than facts, to examine the evidence for and against them, and to develop more balanced and accurate ways of interpreting situations.

The behavioural component, which is often the more powerful of the two, involves changing the behaviours that maintain anxiety. The most important of these is avoidance. Through a process called graded exposure, you systematically re-engage with situations you have been avoiding, starting from the least anxiety-provoking and building up gradually. Each successful exposure provides evidence to the nervous system that the situation is not as dangerous as the anxiety predicted.

What CBT sessions look like

  • Sessions are typically 50 to 60 minutes, weekly or fortnightly
  • The therapist and client work collaboratively, not the therapist diagnosing and the client receiving information
  • Between sessions there are practices and exercises, sometimes called homework, that are central to the work. Therapy that only happens in sessions is less effective than therapy where the skills are practised in daily life
  • Progress is reviewed regularly and the approach is adjusted based on what is and is not working
  • The overall structure moves from understanding the pattern, to developing skills, to applying those skills to increasingly challenging situations

How effective is CBT for anxiety

CBT has the strongest evidence base of any psychological treatment for anxiety disorders. Meta-analyses consistently show large effect sizes, meaning meaningful and clinically significant improvement, across different anxiety presentations. Response rates in clinical trials typically range from 50 to 80 percent, with many people showing substantial or complete remission of symptoms.

CBT is also a skill-building approach, which means the benefits tend to be maintained after therapy ends because the person has developed the tools to continue managing anxiety independently.

Online CBT for anxiety

CBT for anxiety can be delivered effectively online with no reduction in outcomes compared to in-person delivery. This is well-established in the research and reflects the fact that CBT is a structured, skills-based approach that does not require physical presence to be effective.
