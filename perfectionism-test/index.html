Adaptive standards
High standards, low anxiety
Your results suggest your standards are largely driven by genuine values rather than anxiety. You can rest, accept imperfection and recover from criticism without excessive distress. This is the healthy version of caring about quality.
What this looks like
Adaptive perfectionism is associated with high performance, genuine satisfaction in achievement and resilience in the face of setbacks. Your high standards appear to be an asset rather than a burden.
Worth watching
Even adaptive standards can shift toward anxiety-driven perfectionism under high stress. Noticing if rest becomes more difficult or mistakes become more distressing is the early signal.
⚖️
Anxiety mixed in
Standards and anxiety are both at play
Your results suggest your standards are a mix of genuine values and anxiety-driven perfectionism. The anxiety component is producing some real costs: difficulty resting, over-investment in low-stakes tasks and harder-than-necessary recovery from mistakes.

The distinction that matters
The question is not whether you have high standards but whether they can be satisfied. Anxiety perfectionism has a movable goalpost: the standard is always just a bit higher than what you have achieved. Genuine standards can be met.
The highest leverage change
Practising deliberately finishing work that is good rather than perfect, and observing what actually happens, is the most direct way to start updating the beliefs that maintain anxiety-driven perfectionism.

Anxiety-driven perfectionism
Fear is running your standards
Your results suggest your perfectionism is primarily anxiety-driven rather than value-driven. The standards are maintained by fear of failure, fear of judgment and a belief that imperfection has serious consequences. This is exhausting and ultimately impairs rather than improves performance.

The cost at this level
At a high level, anxiety perfectionism produces procrastination through fear of starting, inability to complete because nothing is ever quite ready, exhaustion from the sustained effort, and the loss of genuine satisfaction from any achievement.
What actually changes things
CBT for perfectionism targets the beliefs that maintain it: the catastrophising about consequences of imperfection and the equation of performance with worth. Behavioural experiments, deliberately submitting or finishing something imperfect and observing the actual consequences, are the most directly effective intervention.
Self-compassion is not lowering standards
Research consistently shows that self-compassion is associated with better performance than self-criticism. The intuition that harsh self-criticism is what produces quality is wrong. It produces anxiety that impairs quality.
