    What is perfectionism and when does it become a problem?

    Perfectionism is the tendency to hold very high standards. Not all perfectionism is problematic. Healthy perfectionism involves high standards that are genuinely satisfiable, produce real satisfaction when met, and come from values rather than from fear. Unhealthy or maladaptive perfectionism involves standards driven by anxiety about failure or judgment, where the standard always moves, rest never feels earned, and the brief relief after completion is quickly replaced by anxiety about the next task.

    The distinction matters because the intervention is different. Healthy perfectionism does not require change. Anxiety-driven perfectionism requires addressing the anxiety underneath it, not just trying to lower your standards. For a broader picture of anxiety severity, the anxiety level test covers perfectionism alongside other anxiety dimensions.

    Signs of anxiety-driven perfectionism

    • Procrastination driven by fear of doing things imperfectly
    • Inability to complete and genuinely rest after finishing
    • Disproportionate distress in response to mistakes or criticism
    • The sense that your worth as a person depends on your output
    • Standards that always move: meeting one only reveals the next
    • Difficulty delegating because others will not do it to a sufficient standard

    Frequently asked questions

    What is the difference between healthy and unhealthy perfectionism?

    Healthy perfectionism involves standards that are satisfiable, come from genuine values and produce real satisfaction. Unhealthy perfectionism involves standards driven by fear where completion never feels good enough and worth depends on performance.

    Is perfectionism a form of anxiety?

    Anxiety-driven perfectionism is fundamentally an anxiety pattern. The fear of failure or judgment maintains behaviour that provides short-term anxiety relief but long-term costs.

    Can perfectionism cause procrastination?

    Yes. Fear of doing something imperfectly makes starting it feel more threatening than not starting. Perfectionism-driven procrastination is one of the most common patterns.

    Does therapy help with perfectionism?

    Yes. CBT combining cognitive work with behavioural experiments, deliberately completing and submitting imperfect work, is particularly effective for anxiety-driven perfectionism.
