Why this matters
Emotional exhaustion from anxiety creates a feedback loop: exhaustion reduces the cognitive resources available for managing anxiety, which increases the anxiety, which produces more exhaustion. Addressing this requires working on both the anxiety and the depletion simultaneously.
The most accessible intervention
Sleep quality improvement, regular exercise and reducing the anxiety-driven vigilance that consumes resource between episodes are the most direct routes to restoring reserves.

What this level means
At this level of depletion, the capacity to use the cognitive and behavioural skills that manage anxiety is itself compromised. The depleted nervous system is more reactive, less able to tolerate uncertainty, and less resilient to additional demands. Addressing the depletion is a prerequisite for more effective anxiety management.
What is most important now
Genuine rest and genuine reduction of demands are the priority at this level, alongside professional support. This is not the same as avoidance. It is recognising that a depleted system cannot sustainably engage with the full demands of anxiety treatment until some reserves are restored.
The anxiety vs burnout distinction
At this level, the pattern may have moved from anxiety-driven depletion into burnout territory. The anxiety vs burnout quiz and the burnout guide can help clarify which you are dealing with.
