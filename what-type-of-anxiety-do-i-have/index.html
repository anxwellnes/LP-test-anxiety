Pattern: Generalised anxiety
Your anxiety looks most like GAD
Your responses suggest your anxiety is generalised rather than focused on a specific domain. Pervasive worry across multiple areas, intolerance of uncertainty and persistent background tension are the defining features. This is the most common anxiety pattern.
What this means
Generalised anxiety disorder involves worry that shifts between topics and does not resolve even when immediate concerns are addressed. The anxiety is about uncertainty itself as much as any specific threat.
What helps most
CBT and ACT both have strong evidence for GAD. Intolerance of uncertainty training, scheduled worry time and reducing the reassurance-seeking that temporarily resolves but ultimately maintains the anxiety are the core targets.

Pattern: Social anxiety
Your anxiety looks most like social anxiety
Your responses suggest your anxiety is primarily social, centred on evaluation, judgment and how you come across to others. Social situations, their anticipation and their aftermath occupy most of the anxious thinking.
What this means
Social anxiety involves a specific and intense fear of negative evaluation that produces avoidance of social and performance situations. It is maintained by avoidance and safety behaviours that prevent the natural learning process from reducing the anxiety.
What helps most
CBT with an exposure component is the most effective treatment, particularly the reduction of safety behaviours and attention retraining from internal monitoring to external engagement. The social anxiety guide covers this in detail.

Pattern: Health anxiety
Your anxiety looks most like health anxiety
Your responses suggest health concerns are the primary focus of your anxiety. Whether symptom-focused or more general illness worry, the checking, reassurance-seeking and avoidance cycle maintains the anxiety rather than resolving it.
What this means
Health anxiety involves interpreting normal bodily experience as threatening, seeking reassurance that temporarily resolves but ultimately maintains the anxiety, and avoidance of health-related triggers. CBT is highly effective.
What helps most
Reducing checking and reassurance-seeking, attention refocusing and cognitive restructuring around health threat probability are the core targets.

Pattern: Panic features
Your anxiety includes significant panic features
Your responses suggest you experience acute panic episodes, either unexpected or in specific contexts. The intensity of the physical symptoms and the fear of the episodes themselves are the defining features.
What this means
Panic disorder involves unexpected panic attacks combined with significant worry about future attacks or avoidance of situations associated with them. It responds very well to CBT, particularly interoceptive exposure.
What helps most
Understanding that panic attacks are not medically dangerous, reducing avoidance of triggers and sensations, and building tolerance of the physical sensations through interoceptive exposure are the most effective approaches.
