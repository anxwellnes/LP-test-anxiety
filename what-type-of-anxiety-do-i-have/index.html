Pattern: Generalised anxiety
Your anxiety looks most like GAD
Pervasive worry across multiple areas, intolerance of uncertainty and persistent background tension. This is the most common anxiety presentation.
What this means

Generalised anxiety disorder involves worry that shifts between topics and does not resolve even when immediate concerns are addressed. The anxiety is about uncertainty itself as much as any specific threat.

What helps most

CBT and ACT both have strong evidence for GAD. Intolerance of uncertainty training, scheduled worry time and reducing reassurance-seeking are the core targets.

Pattern: Social anxiety
Your anxiety looks most like social anxiety
Social evaluation, judgment and how you come across to others are the dominant themes. Anticipation and post-event processing are often as anxious as the situations themselves.
What this means

Social anxiety involves a specific and intense fear of negative evaluation that produces avoidance of social and performance situations. It is maintained by avoidance and safety behaviours that prevent the natural learning process from reducing the anxiety.

What helps most

CBT with an exposure component is the most effective treatment, particularly the reduction of safety behaviours and attention retraining from internal self-monitoring to external engagement.

Pattern: Health anxiety
Your anxiety looks most like health anxiety
Health concerns are the primary focus. Whether symptom-focused or illness-focused, the checking and reassurance cycle maintains the anxiety rather than resolving it.
What this means

Health anxiety involves interpreting normal bodily experience as threatening, seeking reassurance that temporarily resolves but ultimately maintains the anxiety, and avoidance of health-related triggers.

What helps most

Reducing checking and reassurance-seeking, attention refocusing and cognitive restructuring around health threat probability are the core targets. CBT is highly effective.

Pattern: Panic features
Your anxiety includes significant panic features
Acute episodes with intense physical symptoms, and often significant anxiety about the episodes themselves, are the defining features.
What this means

Panic disorder involves unexpected panic attacks combined with significant worry about future attacks or avoidance of situations associated with them. It responds very well to CBT, particularly interoceptive exposure.

What helps most

Understanding that panic attacks are not medically dangerous, reducing avoidance of triggers and sensations, and building tolerance of physical sensations through interoceptive exposure are the most effective approaches.
