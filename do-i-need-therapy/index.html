    When does therapy actually help?

    Therapy is not only for people in crisis or with a diagnosed condition. Most people who benefit from therapy are dealing with everyday difficulties that have become hard to manage alone, anxiety, low mood, relationship patterns, stress, life transitions or a persistent sense that something needs to change.

    The question is not whether your problems are serious enough for therapy. The question is whether talking to someone trained to help with exactly this kind of thing would make a difference. For most people who are genuinely struggling, it does.

    Signs that therapy would genuinely help

    • You have been feeling the same way for weeks or months without it improving
    • The difficulty is affecting your work, relationships or daily functioning
    • You have tried to address it yourself and it keeps coming back
    • You find yourself going over the same thoughts or feelings repeatedly
    • You are avoiding things you used to do because of how you feel
    • You feel like you are carrying something you cannot put down

    Signs that self-help may be enough for now

    • The difficulty is recent and clearly linked to a specific situation
    • You have good support around you and are using it
    • Things have been improving gradually on their own
    • The impact on your daily life is mild and manageable

    Frequently asked questions

    Do I need to be in crisis to go to therapy?

    No. Most people who go to therapy are not in crisis. They are dealing with persistent patterns, difficult emotions or situations they want to understand better. Therapy is useful long before things reach a crisis point.

    What if I am not sure therapy will help me?

    That uncertainty is very common. Most therapists offer a first session where you can describe what is going on and ask questions. You do not need to commit to anything. Many people find that even that first conversation is more useful than they expected.

    How is online therapy different from in-person therapy?

    Online therapy offers the same evidence-based approaches as in-person therapy with the added convenience of accessing it from wherever you are. For many people, particularly those with anxiety, the lower barrier to starting is itself significant.

    What other tools can help me understand what I am dealing with?

    If anxiety is part of what you are experiencing, the anxiety level test gives you a full severity score. If you are not sure whether it is anxiety or something else, the anxiety or depression test can help clarify which pattern is dominant.
